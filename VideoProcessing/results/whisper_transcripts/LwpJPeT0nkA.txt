Mealworms, despite their name, aren't worms but the larvae of the darkling beetle, Tenebrio molitor. They start as tiny eggs, hatch into mealworms, and eventually transform into adult beetles. These adaptable creatures can thrive almost anywhere – backyards, farms, and even space. Mealworms have even been sent to the International Space Station to study how they survive in zero gravity. What makes mealworms truly special is their role as natural recyclers. They can eat organic waste like leaves, cardboard, and even styrofoam, breaking it down into nutrients that enrich the soil. Researchers are exploring how mealworms might help solve the global plastic waste problem, as they can digest materials that take decades to decompose. So, what makes these little guys so amazing that more and more people are starting to farm them? In mealworm farms, mealworms are typically kept in shallow trays, or boxes filled with a substrate like bran or oats, which serves as both bedding and food. These trays are stacked in large racks and placed in cool, dark environments. Darkness is important because mealworms are naturally nocturnal and prefer low-light conditions, which help reduce their stress and keep them active. Mealworms are easy to feed. They usually eat grains, plants, and decaying matter, but they can also thrive on a simple vegan diet. This means they don't need much special care. Mealworm beetle mate when the female lays eggs in a warm, dry environment with plenty of food. Mealworms require a warm, dry environment to breed effectively. To breed mealworms and get the larvae, which we need, it's important to provide a substrate like wheat bran or oats for them to lay their eggs. After mating, a female darkling beetle lays 70 to 100 eggs in a dark, moist place with plenty of food. These eggs hatch into creamy white mealworm larvae. The larvae grow to be about half an inch to one inch long, depending on their age. They shed their outer skin, called molting, 10 to 14 times before turning into pupae. The pupal stage can last anywhere from 6 to 300 days, depending on the temperature. Farmers typically feed mealworm larvae by sprinkling food into their trays. The feeding frequency depends on the number of larvae, but generally, food is added daily or every 2 to 3 days to ensure the larvae always have enough nutrition. Usually, mealworm larvae will consume all the food in the tray, but over time, if there isn't enough fresh food or if there are no changes, some larvae may leave a bit of leftover food. Therefore, farmers check regularly and replace the food to ensure the quality and cleanliness of the farming environment.
