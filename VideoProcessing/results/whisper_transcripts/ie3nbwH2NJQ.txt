Hand or machine harvested, they're processed within hours to prevent oxidation. A standout feature of this process is that grapes aren't washed with water. Next, grapes enter massive crushers, handling up to 10,000 pounds per hour. Designed with gentle rollers and rotating paddles, these machines softly press grapes without crushing seeds, a critical detail in winemaking. Crushed seeds release tannins, imparting an unwanted bitter taste that compromises the delicate flavor profile winemakers strive for. The pressed grape juice, or must, flows into large fermentation tanks to develop the wine's signature taste. This process typically lasts 10 to 30 days, depending on grape variety, sugar content, and environmental conditions. Natural yeast triggers a chemical reaction, converting sugar into alcohol and carbon dioxide. As CO2 forms during fermentation, it pushes grape skins to the surface, creating a cap that's regularly pushed down to let the wine absorb color, aroma, and tannins from the skins while preventing bacterial or mold growth. Once fermentation concludes, the wine is clarified and separated from solids, removing skins, seeds, and debris. This is done using filters or specialized equipment, efficiently separating wine from pumice. Filtration enhances clarity and reduces compounds that could cause bitterness or off flavors. Post-filtration, wine moves to aging barrels or further production stages, ready to refine its perfect flavor profile. For premium red wines, filtered wine is transferred to oak barrels for aging, where true magic unfolds. Carefully pumped into barrels, the wine rests for 12 months to over 2 years. Oak aging infuses distinctive woody aromas and softens grape tannins, crafting a smooth, layered texture in every sip.
