 Three things you didn't know about North Rondsey sheep. 1. They're known as the seaweed-eating sheep. During the 1800s, the population of the Scottish island increased, and thus, so did the demand for food. So when these sheep were found to be eating the islander's crop, they were banished to the beaches behind the world's tallest dry stone wall. So, with no grass to eat or crops to pinch, the sheep turned to the gifts of the tide and began eating seaweed to survive. 2. They were the first rescue project of the Rare Breed Survival Trust. For hundreds of years, the breed solely lived on the northernmost of the Orkney Isles. This was a serious danger. Should any disease reach the sheep, they were at high risk of the entire breed being wiped out. So in 1974, Joe Henson flew out to purchase the Ronnies their second island, Linger Home, and he brought some back to found the Bembra flock, who are still alive and well here today at Cotswold Farm Park. 3. Copper is their kryptonite. This is thanks to their seaweed diet and the unique metabolism they evolved. North Rondsey is a very sensitive to copper and will die of copper toxicity if put on the wrong type of grazing. Bonus fact.
