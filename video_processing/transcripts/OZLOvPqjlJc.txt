 Ever wonder what happened to a newborn calf right after it spawned, especially on a large-scale dairy farm? The moment a calf arrives, the team gets to work. The first priority to making sure it's breathing properly and in good health. They clear it's airways, help it to take those all-important first breath, and within no time, the calf is always trying to stand on its own feet. On most modern farms, the calf is gently separated from its own mother within a few hours. This helps protect it from the illness and ensure it receives the right nutrition straight away. The colostrum The colostrum is the cow's first milk, rich in the antibodies. Cows must have it within the first few hours to help build their immune system. Next, the calf is moved into a clean individual bin. These are kept dry, comfortable, and well-ventilated to reduce the risk of infection. Each calf is tagged and recorded. Their health is checked daily temperature, weight, and general condition. They are bottle-feed milker-placer or pasteurized wall milk twice a day, sometimes three times a day. Clear water and starter feed are also provided to help their digestion to develop. Comfort is a key. Beading is regularly replaced depending on the weather, heating, and cool system that might be used. Within a few weeks, the calves grow stronger and begin socializing, getting ready for the next chapter of their lives on the farm.
