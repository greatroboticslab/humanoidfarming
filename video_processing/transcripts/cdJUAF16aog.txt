Education
 Agribusiness and Rural Development Engineering, BSC is an innovative seven-semester long program designed to equip you with the expertise to tackle rural challenges through sustainable development, agricultural economics, and regional management. With their acquired knowledge of agronomic, economic, management, business, analytical, advisory, agro-commerce, agro-marketing, environmental, and regional skills, and deep understanding of the relationship between administrative tasks and the agricultural economy, graduates of this program can carry out professional tasks that meet the market expectations. They have the competence to interpret rural development according to the standards of the European Union. With the necessary skills for planning and implementing rural development programs, they are prepared to pursue their studies in the master's degree. Throughout the program, you will experience a balanced combination of theoretical learning and practical application. In the early semesters, the curriculum builds a strong foundation in sustainable rural development and economic management, providing you with essential insights into the issues facing rural areas today. As you progress, the focus shifts to advanced analysis, planning, and management strategies, culminating in a thesis that synthesizes your acquired knowledge with real-world challenges. A key component of the program is the 12-week field practice, which offers invaluable hands-on experience in actual rural settings, ensuring that you can apply theoretical concepts to practical scenarios effectively. Admission requires a high school diploma and demonstrated proficiency in English. The annual tuition fee is $7,000 USD. Graduates are well prepared for a variety of career opportunities. The wide range of management and rural development skills enable graduates to pursue different careers and adapt their knowledge to different conditions in their home countries. Graduates may find employment at agricultural companies or in public administration.
